# Objection Handling Guide

> Use this when a prospect goes quiet, pushes back, or says "not right now."
> Every line here is copy-paste ready.

---

## The 6 Most Common Objections

---

### 1. "It's too expensive."

**What they really mean:** I don't see the ROI yet. Show me the math.

**Response A (email/DM):**
> "Totally fair — let me run the numbers for your situation.
>
> If your team spends even 5 hours a week on the tasks we'd automate, that's ~20 hours/month. At €25/hour that's €500/month in labour cost — every month, forever.
>
> The build cost is typically €600–900, paid once. Month 2 onwards, you're saving money.
>
> Worth a 20-minute call to see if the numbers make sense for you?"

**Response B (discovery call):**
> "I hear you. Can I ask — what does that task cost you right now in time or staff hours? [Let them answer.] So roughly €X/month. The build is a one-time cost of €Y. You'd break even by month Z. Does that change how you're thinking about it?"

**Key move:** Make them calculate the current cost themselves. People trust their own numbers more than yours.

---

### 2. "AI won't work for my type of business."

**What they really mean:** I've heard AI hype. Prove this is real.

**Response (email/DM):**
> "That's a fair concern — most AI tools are built for tech companies, not for a [plumber / gym / clinic].
>
> Here's what I actually built: [link to demo]. It handles [specific task], [specific task], and [specific task] — no jargon, no IT department needed.
>
> The businesses I work with aren't tech companies. They're exactly like yours. The automation just runs in the background and hands you the result.
>
> What part of your workflow would save you the most time if it ran itself?"

**Key move:** Use the specific vertical demo. Never let "AI won't work" sit unanswered — it's just unfamiliarity.

---

### 3. "I'll just do it myself / use ChatGPT."

**What they really mean:** I think I can figure this out. Convince me otherwise.

**Response:**
> "You absolutely can. ChatGPT is great for one-off tasks.
>
> The difference is: I build systems that run without you. No prompting, no copy-pasting, no remembering to do it. It just happens — lead comes in, gets scored, you get an alert. Appointment booked, reminder goes out automatically.
>
> The build is the hard part. Running it is zero effort.
>
> If you want, I can show you exactly what one looks like — takes 20 minutes."

**Key move:** Don't argue. Acknowledge they can do it, then explain what "done for you" actually means vs DIY.

---

### 4. "I need to think about it."

**What they really mean:** I'm not convinced yet, or I need a nudge.

**Response (same day follow-up):**
> "No pressure at all — take all the time you need.
>
> One thing that might help: here's a quick breakdown of what you'd get [link to service package or one-pager]. If any questions come up, I'm easy to reach.
>
> I'll check back in a week. If it's not a fit, just say the word — no hard feelings."

**Response (7-day follow-up):**
> "Hey [name], just circling back. Still happy to answer any questions or show you a live demo if that would help you decide. No rush either way."

**Key move:** Low pressure, specific offer (demo / breakdown), clear timeline. Don't disappear after "I'll think about it."

---

### 5. "We already have a system / software for that."

**What they really mean:** I don't want to change what's working.

**Response:**
> "That makes sense — if it works, no reason to change it.
>
> What I build usually sits on top of what you already have. No replacing anything. The automation pulls from your existing tools and adds the parts that are still manual.
>
> What does your current setup handle well? And what still falls through the cracks?"

**Key move:** Don't pitch a replacement. Pitch an upgrade layer. Find the gap in what they have.

---

### 6. "Now isn't a good time."

**What they really mean:** There's friction. Either budget cycle, busy season, or a decision-maker isn't involved yet.

**Response:**
> "Completely understand. When would be a better time — is this a month thing, a quarter thing, or more open-ended?
>
> I'll put a note in my calendar and reach out then. If something changes in the meantime, feel free to ping me."

**Follow-up at agreed time:**
> "Hey [name], you mentioned [month/quarter] might work better — just wanted to check in. Has anything changed on your end? Happy to pick up where we left off."

**Key move:** Get a specific date. "Not now" without a date is a soft no. "Not now, but check back in April" is a real lead.

---

## Quick Reference Matrix

| Objection | Core belief to shift | Key move |
|---|---|---|
| Too expensive | Can't see ROI | Make them calculate current cost |
| AI won't work for me | Unfamiliarity / hype scepticism | Show a specific, relevant demo |
| I'll do it myself | DIY feels free | Explain "zero effort to run" |
| Need to think about it | Not convinced yet | Send one-pager + follow up in 7 days |
| Already have a system | Fear of replacement | Position as upgrade layer |
| Not a good time | Friction / timing | Get a specific future date |

## Price Anchoring Script

Use this when price comes up before value is established:

> "Before I get into pricing — can I ask what you're currently spending to handle [the problem]? Either in hours, staff, or tools.
>
> [Let them answer.]
>
> OK. So we're working with [their number] as the baseline. The automation typically costs [range] to build and [monthly cost] to run. What I'd want to make sure is that the math works in your favour before we talk about moving forward."

---

## After a "No"

Don't close the door. Send this:

> "No problem at all — I appreciate you taking the time. If anything changes or you want to revisit it down the road, I'm easy to find.
>
> One small ask: is there anyone else in your network who might find this useful? Even a quick intro would mean a lot."

**Why:** Referrals from "no's" are a real pipeline. People who weren't ready often know someone who is.

---

## Notes for ADHD-Friendly Use

- You don't need to memorise these. Keep the doc open during calls.
- If you blank on a response, buy time: "That's a good question — let me think about that for a second."
- After each call, note which objection came up. Pattern over 5–10 calls = where to improve the pitch.
